An Extended Study of Search User Interface Design

based on the scientific paper: An Extended Study of Search User Interface Design Focused on Hofstede’s Cultural Dimensions by Chessum, K., Liu, H., & Frommholz, I. (2023).

Introduction

Cross-cultural website design has long been discussed, yet little attention
has been paid to culturally sensitive search user interfaces (SUIs).

This study builds on Hofstede’s six cultural dimensions to explore whether these can be meaningfully applied to SUI design (Hofstede, Hofstede, & Minkov, 2010).

Questions

Can cultural dimensions influence user preferences in SUI design?

And to what extent these preferences align with Hofstede’s theory?

Methodology

• Twelve SUI prototypes were created: based on the empirical guidelines from the Hofstede’s dimensions.

• Preferences of the participants were analyzed based on Hofstede’s Index scores.

• Two quantitative online studies were conducted.

Results

Study 2 (N = 25)

Power Distance showed the clearest pattern.
High PD cultures preferred structured, authority-based designs.
For example: Countries like Pakistan and China preferred the version where one search result is clearly highlighted as ‘the best’.

Individualism only confirmed in collectivist countries (e.g., Pakistan, Nepal).

Masculinity was also confirmed.
Competitive, achievement-focused UIs were more popular in masculine cultures.
For example: Participants from Germany and the UK liked seeing search statistics – like how many results were found and how fast – because its measurable.

Uncertainty Avoidance got moderate support.
High UA cultures favored predictable, clear structures.
For example: Countries like Poland preferred search UIs with filters and structure – it gave them a sense of control over the search process.

Long-Term Orientation showed the weakest support, with mixed preferences.

Indulgence Preferences reflected cultural norms around self-expression and leisure.


Power Distance was confirmed in three regions.

Individualism was confirmed in four regions.

Masculinity was only confirmed in China, other countries showed mixed results.

Uncertainty Avoidance had the weakest support overall, only Eastern Europe confirmed.

Long-Term Orientation was confirmed in 3 regions, and showed some partial support in 2 regions.

Indulgence was confirmed in 2 regions, and partially confirmed in another 2 regions.


Conclusion

• Hofstede’s dimensions show partial support in SUI design.

• Power Distance was the most consistently confirmed across both studies.

• Other dimensions (especially UA, LTO, IND) showed divergent or inconsistent results.

• Cultural factors go beyond language and geography — they must be empirically tested.

• Culture-aware design can improve UX, but requires careful adaptation.


Future Research

• Explore under-researched dimensions like LTO and IND.

• Use larger, more balanced samples across regions.

• Develop dimension-specific design heuristics.
